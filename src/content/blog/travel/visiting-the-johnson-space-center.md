This past weekend, I took a quick vacation to visit the Great Wolf Lodge in Houston with my fiancé's inlaws. It's a mainly kid focused place, but it scratched this itch in 10-year-old me, who I think would have absolutely adored it.

Afterward, we stopped by the Johnson Space Center on the way home. Truly tragic that they announced the crew for Artemis III the day *before* we got there, when they were holding an event for it. Still, a lot of cool items there to read about (I love the old 60s-70s technology).

![Johnson Space Center entrance](/images/johnson-space-center-1.webp)

Right out front is Space Center Houston, the visitor center for NASA's Johnson Space Center. There was a cranky person at the front complaining about the state of humanity because of some issue with her ticket.

![Starship Gallery with lunar module replica](/images/johnson-space-center-2.webp)

The Starship Gallery is the first big "wow" moment, with a lunar module hanging overhead and a giant globe of the Moon dominating one wall. It's hard not to just stand there for a minute taking it all in.

![Apollo lunar surface diorama with astronauts and rover](/images/johnson-space-center-3.webp)

One of my favorite exhibits was this lunar surface diorama, complete with a lunar rover and astronauts in full EVA suits. The detail in the staging really makes you feel like you've been dropped onto the Moon in 1971.

![Ceiling](/images/johnson-space-center-4.webp)

![Vintage computer and Apollo-era equipment display](/images/johnson-space-center-5.webp)

This is the kind of stuff I came for: genuinely retro 70s and 80s NASA hardware, like this old monitor and keyboard setup used for analyzing moon rocks. There's something charming about how much of the Space Race ran on this kind of equipment.

![Chalkboard diagram explaining lunar resource utilization](/images/johnson-space-center-6.webp)

I also loved this hand-drawn chalkboard explaining how future lunar bases might extract resources directly from the Moon's soil.

![View through a spacecraft hatch into a training module](/images/johnson-space-center-7.webp)

The tram tour took us through some of the working parts of the campus, including a peek into one of the astronaut training mockups. It's one thing to read about how these modules are laid out, and another to actually look inside one.

![Overhead view of a space station training mockup](/images/johnson-space-center-8.webp)

![Astronaut trainee inside a mockup module](/images/johnson-space-center-9.webp)

Another angle of the same mockup, with a trainee working through the cramped module interior.

![Framed display of Space Shuttle program mission patches](/images/johnson-space-center-10.webp)

This framed collection of mission patches from the entire Space Shuttle program (1981-2011) was beautiful.

![Artemis program entrance with crew portraits](/images/johnson-space-center-11.webp)

The new(er) Artemis exhibit introducing the crew for Artemis II.

![Artemis program welcome display](/images/johnson-space-center-12.webp)

The Artemis welcome wall lays out NASA's plans to return astronauts to the Moon and establish a permanent presence there, framing it as the first step toward Mars. After walking through decades of Apollo and Shuttle history, it's a pretty exciting note to end on.

![Axiom Space Extravehicular Mobility Unit spacesuit display](/images/johnson-space-center-13.webp)

Last but not least, this next-generation Axiom Space spacesuit designed for Artemis astronauts on the lunar surface. Comparing it to the bulky suits from the Apollo era really shows how far the engineering has come, while still solving a lot of the same fundamental problems. Feels like I'm in a sci-fi movie, lol.
